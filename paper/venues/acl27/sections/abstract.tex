\begin{abstract}
Synthetic 3D scenes are increasingly used to evaluate vision-language and
embodied agents, but the assets used in those scenes often undergo material
conversion before they can be rendered or shared across toolchains. This paper
studies MDL-to-UsdPreviewSurface conversion as a controlled within-simulation
perturbation for vision-language grounding evaluation. We first quantify the
conversion on four Isaac Sim assets using paired image quality, rendering, and
feature-similarity metrics, finding high visual fidelity (mean PSNR
35.52\,dB, SSIM 0.990, LPIPS 0.020) and strong CLIP/DINOv2 similarity. We then
extend the route to GRScenes by converting a scratch copy of the benchmark
scenes and running matched original/converted VLM grounding pilots. The current
real-model evidence remains pilot-scale: a 15-pair clean visual-QA pool and a
14-pair material-shift stress pool expose model- and coordinate-protocol
sensitivity rather than supporting a final benchmark claim. The main result is
therefore methodological: visual fidelity alone is not sufficient evidence that
a converted synthetic scene preserves language-grounding behavior. VLM
grounding evaluations should separate clean preservation pools from
material-shift stress pools, freeze coordinate contracts, and register claim
boundaries before reporting downstream robustness.
\end{abstract}
