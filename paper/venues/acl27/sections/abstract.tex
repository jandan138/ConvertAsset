\begin{abstract}
Synthetic 3D scenes are increasingly used to evaluate vision-language and
embodied agents, but the assets used in those scenes often undergo material
conversion before they can be rendered or shared across toolchains. This paper
studies MDL-to-UsdPreviewSurface conversion as a controlled within-simulation
perturbation for vision-language grounding evaluation. We first quantify the
conversion on four Isaac Sim assets using paired image quality, rendering, and
feature-similarity metrics, finding high visual fidelity (mean PSNR
35.52\,dB, SSIM 0.990, LPIPS 0.020) and strong CLIP/DINOv2 similarity. We then
extend the route to GRScenes by converting a scratch copy of the benchmark
scenes and running matched original/converted VLM grounding probes. The
clean-preservation branch remains pilot-scale at 15 visually accepted pairs,
while a frozen 30-pair target-centered material-shift stress set now has canonical Gemma4
predictions and scores: answer accuracy is 30/30 for both material conditions
and normalized-1000 point hits are 27/30 on original renders versus 29/30 on
converted renders. A Qwen2.5-VL diagnostic over the same manifest exposes a
different coordinate preference, with stronger raw pixel-space point scores
than normalized-1000 scores. The main result is therefore methodological:
visual fidelity alone is not sufficient evidence that a converted synthetic
scene preserves language-grounding behavior. VLM grounding evaluations should
separate clean preservation pools from material-shift stress pools, freeze
coordinate contracts, and register claim boundaries before reporting downstream
robustness.
\end{abstract}
