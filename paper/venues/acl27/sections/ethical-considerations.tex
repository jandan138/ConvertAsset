The study uses synthetic 3D assets, not human-subject data. The risks are
practical: converted scenes can carry data bias, license limits, unsafe use
risks, or unsupported generalizations. Authors using them should list provenance, license
terms, filters, and intended use. When
those renders train or evaluate VLMs, authors should report failures that alter
task, safety, texture, or grounding cues, and should not treat success in
synthetic scenes as evidence for real deployment. That distinction marks the
evaluation scope.

We used automated development and manuscript-support tools for implementation
assistance, language editing, audit drafting, and
Figure~\ref{fig:acl_method_chain} schematic drafting. The authors retain final
control over the study setup, evidence selection, reported conclusions, citations, and text.
Figure~\ref{fig:acl_method_chain} orients the evidence chain. Measurements,
experiments, and results come from cited sources, logs, tables, and figures.
Tool output is used as implementation or presentation support, while the
empirical evaluation scope is defined by the recorded evidence.
